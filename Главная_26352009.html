​The Human Brain: A Marvel of Complexity

​The human brain, weighing about 1.4 kg, controls thoughts, emotions, and actions. With billions of neurons, it manages everything from survival to complex thinking—still a mystery to science.

​Neurons

​The brain's primary communicators, transmitting signals throughout the body.

Synapses​

​Junctions where neurons exchange information via chemical and electrical signals.

Neuroplasticity​

​The brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections

​What Are Neurons?

​Neurons are specialized cells that transmit information throughout the nervous system. Each neuron consists of a cell body, dendrites, and an axon. Dendrites receive incoming signals, the cell body processes them, and the axon sends the signals to other neurons or muscles. This communication is essential for all bodily functions, from reflexes to complex thoughts.

​The Cerebral Cortex: Center of Higher Functions

​The cerebral cortex is the brain's outer layer, responsible for higher-order functions such as perception, reasoning, and voluntary movement. Divided into four lobes—frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital—it processes sensory information and facilitates complex cognitive tasks.

​The Cerebellum: Coordinator of Movement​

​Located beneath the cerebral hemispheres, the cerebellum plays a crucial role in motor control. It ensures balance, posture, and coordination, allowing for smooth and precise movements. Though it doesn't initiate movement, it fine-tunes motor activity for accuracy.

​Emotional Regulation

​How the brain processes and controls emotional responses.

​Memory Formation

​The process by which experiences are encoded, stored, and retrieved.

​Sensory Integration​

​The brain's ability to combine information from different senses for a cohesive perception.

​The Hippocampus: Memory's Gatekeeper

​The hippocampus, nestled within the temporal lobe, is integral to memory formation and spatial navigation. It helps convert short-term memories into long-term ones and enables us to orient ourselves in space. Damage to this area can lead to memory impairments and disorientation.
